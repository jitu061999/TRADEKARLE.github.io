ECONOMY UPDATE

India's GDP data slowed to 4.4% in the Oct-Dec quarter of FY23 from 6.3 percent the previous quarter, increasing hopes that the economic picture will impact the RBI's rate action in the upcoming Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meeting in April. The slowing of GDP growth can be attributed in part to the waning of pandemic-induced base effects, revisions to last year's growth, and demand disruption caused by higher interest rates. The GDP growth rate was roughly in accordance with the Central Bank of India's projections.

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) decided by a majority vote to increase the policy repo rate by 25 basis points while keeping a "strong vigil" on the inflation outlook. The RBI has increased its benchmark repo rate by 250 basis points since May of 2022, and the market anticipates another rate rise of 25 basis points to 6.75% in April, which may have an impact on development in the coming months. The rate hike resolution was approved by a 4-2 majority, with Dr. Ashima Goyal and Prof. Jayanth R. Varma voting against it. "Policy rate at 6.5 percent still lags the pre-pandemic level," RBI governor Shaktikanta Das said, adding that underlying inflation will stay sticky.

Data showed that the services sector grew at the quickest rate in 12 years in February, driven by robust demand, as price pressures relaxed further amid modest employment growth and capacity constraints in the country. The seasonally adjusted Purchasing Managers Index (PMI) was 55.3 in February, a four-month low, and slightly lower than 55.4 in January due to increased financing costs and weakness in the sector. India's manufacturing sector maintained strong growth in production and new orders, albeit with a noticeable decline in the rate of foreign sales expansion.

Following recent strong service data and stronger manufacturing PMI data, the balance of risks for the April MPC meeting has shifted towards another increase. However, there were some remarks in the latest Minutes of the MPC meeting that are worth mentioning.

On Feb 2022, when the Minutes of the Monetary Policy Committee Meeting for February 6-8, 2023 was released. Dr. Ashima Goyal comments as mentioned in the meeting minutes, “Central Banks around the world had to raise rates fast to reverse large pandemic time cuts once inflation rose…. Normalization of supply chains, softening commodity prices, and falling global demand are bringing inflation down in most countries… The economy seems to have survived sharp tightening well, as better growth compensated for higher rates... It is better to give time for possible softening of both inflation and growth and effects of past monetary tightening to play out... Although global growth prospects have improved, a slowdown continues in advanced economies. Indian exports are showing signs of strain… There are still only early signs of a revival in private investment, which has yet to come out of a decade-long slowdown… The real rate is already positive and is likely to become more so if inflation falls. The RBI’s average inflation forecast for FY 24 of 5.3 percent gives a real interest rate of almost unity with a repo rate of 6.25 percent.

When the Fed is tightening, interest rates tend to rise more for EMs that have sharp currency depreciation. Intervention in FX markets has lowered rupee volatility and helped maintain independence from the Fed stance. Fed action is due to large excess demand, tight labour markets, and an unprecedented deviation from the inflation target. India does not have these conditions and has the space not to follow the Fed… Substantially hikes above unity trigger a shift to a lower trend of private investment and growth which has seen a revival after years… Excessive front-loading of rate hikes carries the risk of over-shooting”.

According to Jayanth R. Varma, India's economic growth appears to be 'very fragile' and it may fall short of what the country needs to meet the aspirations of its growing workforce. The professor at IIM, Ahmadabad, added how exports are suffering and the private sector finds it difficult to get capital, while the government can't support businesses as it eyes fiscal consolidation. The reality check from Jayanth Varma comes a week after Prime Minister Modi claimed that India is now out of the list of fragile-five economies. PM Modi had also claimed that India has become an anti-fragile economy, which is resilient to headwinds and even creates opportunities in a crisis. Prof. Jayanth R. Varma believes that a repo rate of 6.50% very likely overshoots the policy rate needed to achieve price stability, and further tightening is not desirable. I, therefore, vote against this resolution also.
