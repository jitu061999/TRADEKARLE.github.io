So, the world is divided over whether the Fed will be able to deliver a soft or hard landing, and after recent employment and retail sales numbers, the 'No landing' (no recession) possibility has also emerged. Prior to the publication of January job data, there was a 0% probability of a 50 basis point rate hike, but after the figure was released, the likelihood of a 50 basis point hike increased from 0% to 40%. For example, those who predict a hard landing are refuted by stronger economic data, and rate hikes of more than 25 basis points contradict their view of a hard landing. The positive soft-landing team is being contradicted by slowing demand volume of countries, falling housing-related activity, yield curve inversions, record corporate firings, and so on. The data can continue to lie for a longer period of time, but the economic driver can continue to slow down in the background and throw warning signs one after another until data start to discount in reality, such as oil, freight rates, employment data, and so on, and the balance appears to be tilted towards a hard landing.

• As per Jan Hatzius, “The main obstacle to a soft landing is the historical record. In the three soft landings since World War II—1965, 1984, and 1994—the Fed wasn’t trying to push inflation down; it was merely trying to keep it from going higher. At times like the present, when inflation was too high and the Fed set out to push it lower, a recession always occurred”.

The Fed usually cuts by more than 200 basis points during the recession, followed by 300 basis points the following year. As a result, the Fed appears to be creating a buffer by increasing interest rates to give itself plenty of leeway in the event of a downturn. Interest rates are used not only to reduce inflation but also to promote economic growth. During the 2008 crisis, in reaction to deteriorating economic conditions, the FOMC reduced its federal funds rate target from 4.5 percent at the close of 2007 to 2 percent at the onset of September 2008. As the financial crisis and economic contraction worsened in the autumn of 2008, the FOMC increased interest rate cuts, bringing the rates down to a range of 0 to 25 basis points.
